Why your hiring process is slower than your competitors' — and how to fix it.

Most recruitment bottlenecks are not caused by bad people. They are caused by processes designed for a different era. Here's where to look first.

When a recruitment firm tells me their time-to-fill has crept up, the first instinct is almost always to blame the team. "Sourcing isn't moving fast enough." "The coordinators are overwhelmed." "Clients are slow to respond."

Sometimes that's true. Usually it isn't. In four out of five audits I've run, the slowness wasn't a people problem — it was a process designed for a smaller, simpler version of the business that nobody had bothered to redesign.

Here's where the time is actually leaking, in the order I'd check.

1. The intake handoff nobody owns

The single most common bottleneck I find: the moment a new role lands in the pipeline. The salesperson who closed it knows what the client really wants. The recruiter who'll fill it doesn't. Somewhere in between, a brief gets written, a Slack message gets sent, an email gets forwarded — and 24 to 72 hours quietly disappear before anyone is actually sourcing.

Multiply that across 30 active roles a month and you've lost a full week of pipeline velocity to a handoff nobody is measuring.

The fix is not a meeting. It's a structured intake form that the salesperson can't skip, plus a written rule that says "no role enters the queue without it." Two days back, every month.

2. Manual screening that should be filtered upstream

Coordinators spend an enormous amount of time looking at CVs that should never have reached them. Wrong location. Wrong seniority. Wrong work authorisation. Wrong industry.

You don't need AI to fix this — you need a properly configured filter at the application stage and a screening question set that does the disqualifying work for you. AI helps later, when you want to rank the candidates who do qualify. But before AI, fix the funnel.

If your team is reviewing more than 50 CVs to find one viable candidate, the funnel is broken, not the team.

3. The "waiting on client" black hole

Most pipeline slowdowns blamed on the client are actually slowdowns the firm could have prevented. The client said "send me 3 candidates." You sent 3 on Tuesday. By Friday you've heard nothing. Your team waits. Then waits more. By Monday the candidates have other offers.

The pattern: no follow-up SLA, no automated nudge, no escalation rule. The client isn't ignoring you on purpose — they're busy. Your operations should make it impossible for a candidate to sit untouched in a client's inbox for more than 48 hours.

This is two automations and one dashboard. It is not a strategy retreat.

4. The tooling stack that doesn't talk to itself

The tax on a fragmented tool stack is real and it compounds. ATS in one system, CRM in another, scheduling in a third, contracts somewhere in shared drives, invoicing in QuickBooks, comms in Slack and email and WhatsApp depending on who you're talking to. Every handoff between tools is a place where information dies, gets re-typed, or simply doesn't get passed at all.

You don't fix this by buying one mega-tool. You fix it by being deliberate: pick the systems that are load-bearing, integrate them properly, and stop tolerating the ones nobody is actually using.

Most firms I've worked with are paying for at least three tools they could remove tomorrow. Removing them is faster than adding new ones.

The fix that takes one quarter

None of this requires AI. It requires looking at one workflow at a time, mapping where the handoffs are, and rebuilding the parts where time is leaking.

The order I'd recommend:

  1. Map your full hiring process with timestamps for one role end-to-end. Be honest. The total clock time will surprise you.
  2. Identify the two handoffs where the most time is lost. There are always two that dwarf the rest.
  3. Fix only those two this quarter. Resist the urge to fix everything at once.

Bottom line: your competitors aren't faster because they have better people or better tools. They're faster because they redesigned the boring parts of the process you've been tolerating. That redesign is the most underpriced advantage in recruitment right now.
